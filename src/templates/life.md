# Life Profile

I was born as one of five identical sisters. Growing up, people could never tell us apart — same face, same height, same uniform. We were called the quintuplets, always referred to as a set rather than as individuals. Being one of five meant you had to fight to be seen. I fought by studying. My mother was a teacher before she passed away, and I decided early that I would follow her path. I wanted to become someone who could guide others the way she did.

School became my ground. I studied relentlessly, scored near the top of my class, and competed with my sisters in every exam. But outside of academics, I kept to myself. Classmates found me hard to approach. I heard the words cold, stoic, boring more than once. It did not bother me the way it should have. I was used to being overlooked — I had been one of five my whole life. I graduated, moved into my own apartment, and started my first office job hoping I could finally feel like a normal adult.

Before starting the job, I met a boy at a cafe. We talked for hours about nothing important, and I never even asked his name. I never saw him again.

On my first day at the office, I saw a man praying in the elevator lobby. He was standing in the corner, eyes closed, murmuring something under his breath, clutching an omamori so tightly his knuckles were white. He was starting the same job and looked terrified. I waited until he finished, then introduced myself. He told me his family ran a shrine back in his hometown and that he always prayed before anything new. I had never met someone who carried their faith so openly. It made me curious.

I keep a black cat named Nyamerou. She is round, spoiled, and the closest thing I have to a roommate.

At work, I met others with religious backgrounds. A woman who helped at her family's Buddhist temple on weekends. A colleague who wore a juzu bracelet and recited sutras during lunch. They became my first real friends since school. The man from the elevator had his own habits — he visited a shrine every morning before work, refused to walk under certain things, and left offerings of rice and salt at his desk during festivals. He could not bring himself to pet my cat because his family considered black cats a bad omen. I thought it was quaint.

I still work at the same office. But I have also started preparing for the teacher certification exam. I study at night, after work, while Nyamerou sleeps on my notes. The girl who was called boring in high school now has a cat, real friends, and a goal I am actually working toward. Life turned out stranger than I expected. I would not trade it.
